# Problem

## Are you evaluating a number—or a formula?

A salary can look clear while the economics remain hidden.

The real decision is not simply whether **$300,000** is competitive. It is whether the complete arrangement pays fairly for the work expected, defines what counts, reconciles predictably, and avoids hidden downside.

**Start here:** Do not evaluate the offer until you can explain, in plain language, how each dollar is earned and what could reduce, delay, recoup, or exclude it.

---

# Context

Keep these materials together before comparing an offer:

- Written offer and employment or contractor agreement.
- Full compensation plan and all incorporated policies.
- Expected clinical schedule, sites, call, supervision, and administrative work.
- Productivity definitions and source tables.
- Quality measures and scoring rules.
- Benefits, expenses, leave, and malpractice terms.
- Reconciliation timing, sample calculations, and dispute process.

A headline number without this context is not yet an evaluable business offer.

# Big Picture · 30 sec

## A compensation number is not a compensation formula.

### The visual

**Total physician value**

= **Guarantee**  
+ **Productivity**  
+ **Quality**  
+ **Call, leadership, teaching, supervision, and other work**  
+ **Benefits and other economic value**  
− **Thresholds, exclusions, unreimbursed costs, repayment risk, and lost time**

### Three things physicians commonly miss

1. **The guarantee may be only the floor.** The threshold and conversion rate determine whether additional work pays.
2. **Attribution determines whether your work counts.** A generous rate means little if services, locations, modifiers, APP work, or delayed claims are excluded.
3. **Reconciliation reveals the real plan.** Timing, corrections, deficits, and repayment rules determine whether the formula behaves as expected.

### Do this now

Ask for:

- the complete written formula and definitions;
- one worked example using the activity expected for your role; and
- recent de-identified sample reconciliations if the organization can lawfully provide them.

> **I didn’t know that:** The same headline salary can produce very different economics when the threshold, attribution rules, work expectations, and reconciliation differ.

**Next:** Compare the six terms that actually shape the offer.

# Brief Overview · 2 min

## Compare six dimensions—not salary alone.

### 1. Guarantee

Clarify:

- amount;
- duration;
- reduction triggers;
- whether it is salary, draw, advance, or recoverable guarantee;
- what happens after the guarantee ends.

### 2. Productivity

Identify:

- unit: wRVUs, collections, encounters, sessions, panels, or another measure;
- threshold;
- conversion rate or percentage;
- tiers, caps, floors, and exclusions;
- payment and reconciliation timing.

### 3. Attribution

Ask exactly what counts:

- personally performed services;
- split or shared services;
- APP supervision;
- procedures at other locations;
- services billed later;
- modifiers, global periods, denials, and corrected claims;
- quality, teaching, research, or administrative work.

### 4. Adjustments

Define:

- quality incentives or withholds;
- call pay;
- leadership or medical-director compensation;
- citizenship or committee expectations;
- penalties, chargebacks, and clawbacks.

### 5. Work expectations

Compare:

- clinical sessions;
- call frequency;
- locations and travel;
- supervision;
- inbox and after-hours burden;
- administrative time;
- leave and coverage;
- teaching and research expectations.

### 6. Evidence and control

Request:

- a worked example;
- reconciliation schedule;
- access to source data;
- correction and dispute process;
- audit rights;
- employer amendment rights;
- what happens after termination.

## Same salary. Different economics.

### Offer A

- $300,000 guarantee
- transparent threshold
- defined conversion rate
- quarterly reconciliation
- clear attribution
- limited unilateral amendment rights

### Offer B

- $300,000 guarantee
- higher or undefined threshold
- unclear exclusions
- annual reconciliation
- broad employer discretion
- repayment exposure

The offers do not have the same value merely because the guarantee matches.

### Three questions to bring to the next conversation

1. **What is the exact threshold and how was it selected?**
2. **What is the conversion rate or percentage, and can it change?**
3. **Which work is credited to me, when is it credited, and how can I verify it?**

> **That was useful:** You now have a repeatable framework for comparing offers without relying on the headline number.

**Next:** Stress-test the formula before you accept it.

# Deep Dive · 5 min

## Step 1 — Translate the formula into one sentence

You should be able to say:

> “I receive a guarantee of ___ for ___ months. After ___ units, I receive ___ per additional unit. The organization credits ___, excludes ___, reconciles every ___, and handles deficits or overpayments by ___.”

If you cannot complete that sentence, the plan is not yet clear enough to evaluate.

## Step 2 — Run three scenarios

Test the arrangement at:

- **Low activity:** below expected volume because of ramp-up, leave, staffing, payer delays, or reduced access.
- **Expected activity:** the organization’s stated workload and assumptions.
- **High activity:** strong production with realistic capacity, staffing, call, and quality constraints.

For each scenario, calculate:

- total cash compensation;
- timing of payment;
- amount at risk;
- benefits and expenses;
- unreimbursed work;
- deficit or repayment exposure.

## Step 3 — Separate compensation units from reimbursement

CMS uses work, practice-expense, and malpractice RVUs—adjusted geographically and converted into payment rates—when calculating Medicare Physician Fee Schedule payment. Employers may separately use **wRVUs** or another measure to calculate physician compensation.

The employer’s compensation conversion rate is therefore not automatically the Medicare conversion factor, and the two systems should not be treated as interchangeable.

## Step 4 — Find attribution leakage

Ask what happens when:

- a claim is submitted late;
- a code is corrected;
- a payer denies or downcodes a service;
- work occurs at another site;
- an APP participates;
- the physician covers another clinician;
- a procedure falls in a global period;
- a patient is seen but revenue is not collected;
- an employer changes coding, scheduling, staffing, or payer contracts.

A formula can appear generous while operational rules prevent the physician from receiving credit.

## Step 5 — Examine reconciliation and downside

Clarify:

- monthly, quarterly, or annual timing;
- access to source data;
- lag and correction periods;
- whether deficits carry forward;
- whether overpayments are recouped;
- whether the guarantee is recoverable;
- what happens at termination;
- who resolves disputes;
- how long corrections remain open.

## Step 6 — Value non-salary economics

A fair comparison includes:

- retirement contributions;
- health and disability benefits;
- malpractice and tail coverage;
- CME and professional expenses;
- paid leave;
- schedule control;
- call burden;
- travel and multiple sites;
- support staff and capacity;
- ownership or ancillary opportunities;
- tax and expense differences between employee and contractor structures.

Do not assume a higher contractor rate is automatically more valuable than employee compensation. Worker classification and tax treatment depend on the facts and circumstances, not merely the contract label.

## Step 7 — Identify professional-review triggers

### Consider legal review when:

- restrictive covenants, termination, repayment, indemnification, dispute, amendment, ownership, or compensation-plan incorporation is unclear;
- state law materially affects enforceability;
- the agreement permits broad unilateral changes;
- compensation is connected to referrals or complex ownership arrangements.

### Consider tax or accounting review when:

- comparing W-2 and 1099 structures;
- evaluating entity structure, estimated taxes, benefits, deductions, or retirement plans;
- income timing, deferred compensation, or multi-state work is involved.

### Consider valuation or compliance review when:

- fair market value, commercial reasonableness, ownership distributions, ancillaries, or referral-sensitive arrangements are material.

BOMSociety should help you recognize these issues earlier and use professional time more efficiently—not pretend individualized expertise is unnecessary.

> **I wish someone had taught me this years ago:** The strongest negotiation often begins before discussing the number—by making the formula, assumptions, data, and downside visible.

# Conversation Scripts

## Request the complete formula

> “Please provide the complete compensation formula and definitions in writing, including the guarantee, productivity measure, threshold, conversion rate or percentage, attribution rules, quality terms, call and leadership payments, reconciliation schedule, deficit or repayment treatment, and a worked example using the activity expected for this role.”

## Ask for the assumptions behind the number

> “To compare the offer accurately, I would like to understand the workload and assumptions behind the compensation, including clinical schedule, call, sites, supervision, administrative expectations, leave, support, and how credited work is reconciled.”

## Ask about changes

> “Which parts of the formula can the organization change, what notice is required, and what happens if the compensation plan conflicts with the agreement?”

## Preserve professional-review efficiency

> “I would like enough detail to understand the business terms before deciding which items require legal, tax, valuation, accounting, or benefits review.”

# Common Mistakes

1. Comparing guarantees without comparing workload.
2. Treating an employer wRVU conversion rate as though it were CMS reimbursement.
3. Ignoring attribution and operational capacity.
4. Accepting undefined quality measures or employer discretion.
5. Failing to model the end of the guarantee.
6. Overlooking deficits, draws, repayment, or clawbacks.
7. Comparing W-2 and 1099 pay without taxes, benefits, expenses, and classification risk.
8. Waiting until after signing to request a worked example.
9. Assuming a benchmark proves fairness without examining year, specialty, geography, sample, and role.
10. Hiring a professional before organizing the business questions—then paying them to reconstruct facts the physician could have gathered first.
