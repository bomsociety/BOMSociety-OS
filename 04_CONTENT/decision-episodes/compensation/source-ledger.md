## Claim ledger — verified primary sources

### 1. RVUs are not one undifferentiated number

**Supported claim:** Under the Medicare Physician Fee Schedule, service payment reflects three RVU components: physician work, practice expense, and malpractice expense. Geographic practice cost indices adjust those components, and a conversion factor turns adjusted RVUs into payment rates.

**Why physicians need this:** Employment compensation commonly refers to **work RVUs (wRVUs)**, while Medicare payment uses a broader formula. A physician should not assume the employer’s productivity formula is identical to Medicare reimbursement.

**Source:** Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services, “Physician Fee Schedule.”  
https://www.cms.gov/cms-guide-medical-technology-companies-and-other-interested-parties/payment/physician-fee-schedule

**Source:** CMS, “PFS Look-Up Tool Overview.”  
https://www.cms.gov/medicare/physician-fee-schedule/search/overview

**Source type:** Federal primary source  
**Currentness:** Reviewed in 2026  
**Planned use:** 30-second visual distinction; 2-minute terminology; 5-minute explanation and references.

### 2. Conversion factors and payment rules change

**Supported claim:** CMS updates Physician Fee Schedule values and payment rules through annual rulemaking and periodic relative-value releases. For 2026, CMS implemented separate conversion factors for qualifying APM participants and non-qualifying clinicians.

**Why physicians need this:** Contract language that points to an external schedule, code set, benchmark year, or employer-controlled conversion factor needs a clear update rule. The number can change even when the contract headline does not.

**Source:** CMS, “Calendar Year 2026 Medicare Physician Fee Schedule Final Rule.”  
https://www.cms.gov/newsroom/fact-sheets/calendar-year-cy-2026-medicare-physician-fee-schedule-final-rule-cms-1832-f

**Source:** CMS, “RVU26C — July 2026 release.”  
https://www.cms.gov/medicare/payment/fee-schedules/physician/pfs-relative-value-files/rvu26c

**Source type:** Federal primary source  
**Currentness:** 2026  
**Planned use:** Deep Dive warning: define source, year, update timing, and amendment rights.

### 3. Employee versus independent-contractor classification is fact-specific

**Supported claim:** Worker classification depends on the facts and circumstances, including behavioral control, financial control, and the relationship of the parties. It is not determined solely by the contract label or payment method.

**Why physicians need this:** A 1099 rate should not be compared with W-2 salary as though the tax, benefits, control, expenses, and risk structures were equivalent.

**Source:** Internal Revenue Service, “Independent contractor (self-employed) or employee?”  
https://www.irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/independent-contractor-self-employed-or-employee

**Source:** IRS, “Topic no. 762, Independent contractor vs. employee.”  
https://www.irs.gov/taxtopics/tc762

**Source:** IRS Publication 15-A (2026), Employer’s Supplemental Tax Guide.  
https://www.irs.gov/publications/p15a

**Source type:** Federal primary source  
**Currentness:** 2025–2026  
**Planned use:** Comparison tool; checklist; tax-professional referral trigger.

### 4. Medicare payment and employer compensation are different systems

**Supported claim:** Medicare Physician Fee Schedule amounts reflect work, practice expense, malpractice expense, geography, and a conversion factor. An employer may independently use wRVUs or another productivity measure to calculate physician compensation.

**Editorial inference:** Because an employer compensation formula is contractual and organizational rather than a CMS payment formula, physicians must request the employer’s actual definitions, attribution rules, thresholds, exclusions, reconciliation method, and worked examples. This inference will be labeled as practical guidance, not attributed to CMS.

**Supporting sources:** CMS PFS pages listed above.

## Canonical content blueprint

### Big Picture · 30 sec

**Job:** Reveal the blind spot and produce one immediate action.

**Core insight:** A compensation number is not a compensation formula.

**Visual:**

> Total compensation = guarantee + productivity + quality + call/leadership + benefits/other value − thresholds, exclusions, costs, and repayment risk

**Three high-value facts:**

1. The guarantee may be only one component.
2. Attribution and thresholds determine which work earns additional pay.
3. Reconciliation shows what the formula actually produced.

**Immediate action:** Request the complete written formula, all definitions, one worked example using expected activity, and recent de-identified reconciliation examples where the employer can lawfully provide them.

**Safety refinement:** Do not imply an employer can or should provide another physician’s identifiable compensation records.

### Brief Overview · 2 min

**Job:** Teach the comparison framework.

**Required framework:** Compare offers across six dimensions rather than salary alone:

1. **Guarantee:** amount, duration, reduction triggers, repayment or draw treatment.
2. **Productivity:** unit, threshold, rate, tiers, caps, floors, timing.
3. **Attribution:** which services and clinicians count; timing and correction rules.
4. **Adjustments:** quality, call, leadership, citizenship, penalties, withholds.
5. **Work expectations:** clinical time, call, sites, supervision, administration, leave.
6. **Evidence:** worked example, reconciliation schedule, dispute process, amendment rights.

**Original comparison example:** Two offers with the same $300,000 guarantee but different thresholds, conversion factors, attribution rules, and reconciliation schedules can have materially different economics. The example must remain illustrative and avoid implying a universal market rate.

### Deep Dive · 5 min

**Job:** Leave the physician ready to act.

**Required sections:**

- Translate the formula into plain English.
- Test the formula with low, expected, and high activity scenarios.
- Separate compensation units from payer reimbursement.
- Identify attribution gaps and excluded work.
- Evaluate reconciliation, repayment, and dispute rules.
- Value benefits, schedule, call, administrative work, and ownership separately.
- Compare W-2 and 1099 structures without presenting individualized tax advice.
- Identify legal, tax, valuation, or accounting referral triggers.
- Provide questions, scripts, checklist, calculator, and references.

## Calculator specification v2

The current calculator is a minimal illustration. The production calculator must support:

- Guarantee.
- Productivity unit: wRVU, collections, encounters, sessions, or custom.
- Credited activity.
- Threshold.
- Conversion rate or percentage.
- Tiered rates where applicable.
- Quality incentive or withhold.
- Call and leadership pay.
- Benefits estimate entered by the user, not assumed by the system.
- Employee versus contractor comparison inputs.
- Reconciliation frequency.
- Repayment or negative-balance treatment.
- Low / expected / high scenario comparison.
- Clear assumptions and formula trace.
- Exportable comparison summary.

The calculator must never claim to determine fair market value, legality, tax treatment, or the “correct” offer.

## Checklist specification

The downloadable checklist must include:

- Complete written formula and definitions.
- Guarantee duration and transition.
- Productivity unit and source.
- Threshold and conversion rate.
- Attribution rules and correction process.
- Quality measures and ability to influence them.
- Call, leadership, teaching, research, supervision, and administrative pay.
- Benefits and expenses.
- Workload and location expectations.
- Reconciliation schedule and sample calculation.
- Deficit, repayment, draw, and clawback rules.
- Employer amendment rights.
- Dispute and audit process.
- Referral triggers for lawyer, CPA/tax adviser, valuation specialist, or benefits adviser.

## Script specification

**Request for formula:**

“Please provide the complete compensation formula and definitions in writing, including the guarantee, productivity measure, threshold, conversion rate or percentage, attribution rules, quality terms, call and leadership payments, reconciliation schedule, deficit or repayment treatment, and a worked example using the activity expected for this role.”

**Request for comparability:**

“To compare the offer accurately, I would like to understand the workload and assumptions behind the compensation, including clinical schedule, call, sites, supervision, administrative expectations, leave, and how credited work is reconciled.”

**Professional-review boundary:**

“I would like enough detail to understand the business terms before deciding which items require legal, tax, valuation, or benefits review.”
